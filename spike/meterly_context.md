# Meterly — assumed vendor identity for the spike (fictional)

**Product:** usage-based billing and subscription infrastructure for B2B software
companies. Metering, rating, invoicing, revenue recognition, dunning — the billing
stack between "product emits usage events" and "finance closes the books."

**ICP:** B2B software companies, 11–1,000 employees, US/UK/Canada. Any company that
bills customers for software qualifies at some level; how they bill determines the
message.

**Why segments matter to Meterly's GTM team:** the pain and the pitch differ
completely by business model —
- consumption-priced infrastructure (APIs, data, AI inference) → metering accuracy,
  rating complexity, usage visibility for customers
- seat-based SaaS → plan management, upgrades/downgrades, proration
- enterprise contract sellers → custom terms, rev-rec, procurement-grade invoicing
- agencies/services firms → retainers and project invoicing (weak fit; different
  product entirely — deprioritize or disqualify)
- legacy license sellers → the migrate-to-subscription story

**Sales motion:** outbound-heavy; the team writes ~1 sequence per segment and wants
to know which segments exist, who's in each, which are worth deeper enrichment, and
which to suppress.

## Blessed seed hypotheses (written before the run — canaries, not constraints)

1. **Agencies in SaaS clothing** — services/consultancy revenue models hiding under
   the "computer software" industry label. Only visible in prose. Expect large,
   especially 11–50. Messaging: retainer invoicing or disqualify.
2. **Usage-metered infrastructure sellers** — products that inherently sell
   consumption units. Only visible in product descriptions. Meterly bullseye.
3. **Legacy license holdouts** — on-prem/perpetual-license vocabulary; corroborate
   with early founded years and older site tech (held out of the embedding, used as
   independent verification). Messaging: migration to subscription.

**Ambiguity-layer prediction:** the noise/outlier bucket should catch the
domain-drift cases (repurposed domains where structured data and site content
disagree, e.g. the Nobarlive find).
